Dataset Overview
Our dataset, dating back to 1988, is comprised of four main databases: Cleveland, Hungary, Switzerland, and Long Beach V. These databases collectively contain 76 attributes, each serving a specific purpose in the diagnosis of heart diseases. The most crucial attribute, the "target" field, indicates the presence of heart disease in the patient, denoted as an integer value where 0 signifies no disease and 1 represents the presence of disease.
Key Attributes
To make precise predictions, our system employs a subset of 14 essential attributes from the extensive dataset. These key attributes include:
Age: The age of the patient in years.
Sex: The gender of the patient (1 for male, 0 for female)
Chest Pain Type: A categorical attribute reflecting the type of chest pain experienced by the patient (values 1-4).
Resting Blood Pressure: The patient's resting blood pressure in mm Hg.
Serum Cholestoral: The serum cholesterol level in mg/dl.
Fasting Blood Sugar: Indicates whether fasting blood sugar is greater than 120 mg/dl (1 for true, 0 for false).
Resting Electrocardiographic Results: Results of resting electrocardiogram (values 0, 1, 2).
Maximum Heart Rate Achieved: The highest heart r ate achieved during testing.
About Diabetes
Diabetes is a chronic medical condition characterized by elevated blood sugar levels, also known as hyperglycemia. It occurs when the body either cannot produce enough insulin or cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. Insulin is a hormone that regulates blood sugar and allows cells to utilize glucose for energy. There are two main types of diabetes: Type 1 diabetes, which is usually diagnosed in childhood or adolescence and results from the body's inability to produce insulin; and Type 2 diabetes, which is more common and typically develops in adults, often due to lifestyle factors such as poor diet, lack of exercise, and obesity.
About our Ai Model
The Decision Tree Classifier is a supervised machine learning algorithm used for both classification and regression tasks. In the context of diabetes risk detection, the algorithm is trained on a dataset comprising various features associated with diabetes, such as age, BMI, family history, blood pressure, and glucose levels. Through a process of recursive partitioning, the algorithm creates a decision tree by identifying the most significant features and their respective thresholds, enabling accurate classification of individuals into risk categories.
Model Accuracy
Precision and recall are essential metrics in evaluating the performance of classification models, including the Decision Tree Classifier, for diabetes risk detection. our model's impressive precision and recall signify its accuracy in identifying individuals at risk of diabetes, ensuring that healthcare providers can make confident, targeted interventions based on the model's predictions. This high level of accuracy is instrumental in improving the effectiveness of preventive healthcare strategies and ultimately enhancing patient outcomes.

About our Dataset
The Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) is a health-related telephone survey that is collected annually by the CDC. Each year, the survey collects responses from over 400,000 Americans on health-related risk behaviors, chronic health conditions, and the use of preventative services. It has been conducted every year since 1984. For this project, a csv of the dataset available on Kaggle for the year 2015 was used. This original dataset contains responses from 441,455 individuals and has 330 features. These features are either questions directly asked of participants, or calculated variables based on individual participant responses.
